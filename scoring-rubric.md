# Strategy Scoring Rubric

Use this rubric to judge whether an idea is strong enough to become the foundation for a real organization.

Scores are not a substitute for judgment. They are a way to make disagreements clearer.

| Criterion | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Impact size | Helps very few people or produces tiny gains. | Helps a narrow group with modest gains. | Could create meaningful gains for a defined group. | Could create large gains for many people. | Could shift income or opportunity at very large scale. |
| Scalability | Depends on rare conditions or constant manual effort. | Can grow a little but likely breaks under scale. | Can scale within a niche or region. | Has a credible path across markets or countries. | Could scale globally without losing its core benefit. |
| Tractability | No clear path to execution. | Possible only with major luck or permission. | A small pilot is possible with known constraints. | Execution is hard but realistic with a capable team. | Clear first steps, clear users, and manageable barriers. |
| Defensibility | Easy to copy, capture, or neutralize. | Some weak protection from brand or relationships. | Moderate protection through trust, data, process, or network effects. | Strong protection from reputation, standards, or infrastructure. | Hard to displace because the model becomes trusted public infrastructure. |
| Automation resilience | Likely made obsolete by AI or automation. | Highly exposed to falling prices or task replacement. | Some exposed parts, but the model can adapt. | Benefits from automation or shifts toward harder-to-automate value. | Becomes more important as automation spreads. |
| Political feasibility | Requires political changes that are very unlikely. | Faces strong opposition with few allies. | Politically possible in limited settings. | Can attract a workable coalition. | Fits a clear political opening or avoids major political dependency. |
| Revenue realism | No credible way to fund operations. | Revenue depends mostly on donations or goodwill. | Some plausible revenue, but unit economics are uncertain. | Revenue model is credible with testable assumptions. | Strong alignment between revenue, mission, and growth. |
| Measurement clarity | Success cannot be measured. | Measures are vague or easy to manipulate. | A few useful metrics exist. | Clear metrics can guide decisions and public accountability. | Measurement is simple, hard to fake, and tied to real equality gains. |
| Movement potential | Inspires almost no participation beyond the team. | Some supporters may care, but the story is weak. | A focused community could form. | The idea can attract workers, experts, builders, and funders. | The idea can become a broad public cause without losing precision. |
| Failure honesty | Avoids or hides the hardest objections. | Names risks but treats them lightly. | Acknowledges major failure modes. | Builds tests around the hardest objections. | Clearly states what would kill the idea and actively looks for that evidence. |

Every scored strategy should end with:

- Best next experiment:

## How To Use The Rubric

Do not average the scores too quickly. A strategy with one fatal weakness may be worse than a strategy with several medium weaknesses.

The most important question is not "Does this sound good?" The most important question is "Would this still be worth building after its strongest criticism is taken seriously?"
