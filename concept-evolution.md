# Concept Evolution

## Plain-Language Introduction

This is the more technical strategy record for Global Equality Lab.

If you are new to the project, you may want to read [Start Here](/start-here) or the [plain-English summary](/plain-english-summary) first. This page keeps the deeper reasoning: what the original idea was, why it changed, what criticisms matter, and how the strategy evolves under pressure.

The purpose is not to defend one favorite idea forever. The purpose is to track how the idea changes under criticism until the lab can choose an approach strong enough to build a real organization around.

## From Digital Rent Redistribution to a Multi-Lever Equality Framework

The original concept centered on digital rent redistribution: identifying the concentrated economic rents created by platforms, data advantages, automation, networks, intellectual property, and digital infrastructure, then redirecting some of those gains toward people excluded from the upside.

That remains an important strategic lens. Digital rents are growing, globally uneven, and often produced through collective social inputs that are not compensated proportionally. But a public strategy laboratory should not begin with a single mechanism as its final answer.

Global equality is too complex for a one-lever theory of change.

## Why Expand the Frame?

Digital rent redistribution raises useful questions:

- Who captures value from shared digital systems?
- What forms of ownership, taxation, licensing, data governance, or dividend design could distribute that value more fairly?
- How can benefits cross borders rather than remain inside wealthy jurisdictions?

It also leaves open harder questions:

- What if the highest-leverage equality path is not redistribution after value capture, but changing who owns productive assets before capture happens?
- What if the binding constraint is political capacity, not technical design?
- What if health, education, migration, climate adaptation, labor power, or institutional trust produce larger equality gains than digital-sector reform?
- What if the best strategy combines several modest levers into a compounding architecture?

## The Open Multi-Lever Framework

The lab will evaluate equality strategies across multiple levers:

| Lever | Core Question | Example Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ownership | Who owns the assets that generate future wealth? | Cooperative, public, trust, or commons-based ownership models |
| Redistribution | How are concentrated gains shared after capture? | Taxes, dividends, royalties, and global transfer mechanisms |
| Market Design | Which rules shape bargaining power and access? | Procurement, labor standards, competition policy, and platform governance |
| Public Goods | Which shared investments unlock broad capability? | Health, education, infrastructure, climate resilience, and open knowledge |
| Governance | Who has voice over systems that shape their lives? | Participatory institutions, transnational accountability, and civic power |
| Technology | Which tools reduce exclusion or increase dependency? | Open infrastructure, data rights, automation policy, and inclusive AI |
| Culture | Which narratives make equality politically durable? | Public imagination, moral framing, and coalition language |

## Evaluation Criteria

Each proposed strategy should be assessed for:

- Scale: how many people could benefit, and how deeply?
- Tractability: what would make implementation possible?
- Neglectedness: why is this not already adequately pursued?
- Durability: would gains persist across political and market cycles?
- Transferability: can the approach cross borders and contexts?
- Risk: who could be harmed, excluded, or made dependent?
- Evidence: what would change our mind?

## Near-Term Research Agenda

1. Map candidate equality levers and the mechanisms by which they might scale.
2. Create debate prompts that force each lever to face its strongest objections.
3. Compare digital rent redistribution against alternative or complementary strategies.
4. Develop a public scorecard for strategic hypotheses.
5. Preserve revisions so the lab can show how its thinking changes over time.

## Current Position

Digital rent redistribution is a promising entry point, not the whole map.

The lab will keep that concept in view while building a broader framework capable of discovering unexpected, compound, and globally relevant paths to equality.
