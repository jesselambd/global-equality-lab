# Plain-English Summary

## The Current Leading Idea

The current leading hypothesis is fair-paid, radically transparent global remote hiring into lower-income countries.

That is a long phrase. The simple version is this:

Companies in rich countries already hire workers in lower-income countries to save money. Too often, the worker receives only a small share of the value, while intermediaries keep the system hard to inspect.

A fairer model would make the split transparent.

## How It Might Work

A client in a rich country pays for remote work.

The organization publishes:

- what the client pays
- what the worker receives
- what the organization keeps
- what services or protections the organization provides

The worker should earn life-changing wages by local standards. The client can still save money compared with hiring domestically. The organization keeps enough to operate, protect quality, and grow without hiding the economics.

The point is not to make low wages look generous. The point is to test whether cross-border hiring can move more of the value to workers while still being practical for clients.

## Why This Could Matter

Remote work is one of the few ways income can cross borders quickly without requiring migration.

If the model worked, it could:

- raise incomes for workers in lower-income countries
- give clients a clear ethical alternative to opaque outsourcing
- prove that transparent labor-market design can compete
- create public data about what fairer cross-border hiring looks like
- become a stepping stone toward larger equality strategies

## Why It Might Fail

This is not the final answer.

The idea may fail for serious reasons:

- AI may reduce demand for many remote digital roles.
- Clients may choose the cheapest option even when the split is unfair.
- The model may help only a small number of already skilled workers.
- Quality control, trust, training, and management may be harder than expected.
- Local wage effects could be complicated.
- A talent marketplace may be less powerful than an ownership, policy, auditing, or movement strategy.

Those objections are not side notes. They are central to the lab.

## What Is Being Tested

The lab is testing whether this idea is strong enough to build an organization around.

Key questions include:

- Can it produce measurable income gains within 12 months?
- Can it protect workers while still attracting clients?
- Can it scale without depending on charity or guilt?
- Can it survive AI-driven changes in the labor market?
- Is a marketplace the right form, or would certification, auditing, policy, ownership, or public advocacy be stronger?

## Current Status

Fair-paid transparent remote hiring is the leading hypothesis, not the conclusion.

The lab is open to better ideas. A stronger mechanism should replace this one if it is more scalable, more realistic, more durable, or more likely to reduce global inequality.
